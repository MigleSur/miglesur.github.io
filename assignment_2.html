How has the weather changed over the past 100 years?

The plot shows mean yearly temperature in two cities: Kaunas (Lithuania) and Madrid (Spain).
The plot was made by adapting the template provided by bl.ocks.org.
The data was prepared with R, the script which was used is here.

Two data sets were used for visualizations with the same time interval (1953-2015) for both cities (such short time period was used because the bigger data set was not available for Madrid). For the first plot the last column of the data sets was used as it shows the mean yearly temperature. The line plot shows how the mean yearly temperature changed since 1953 in Madrid and Kaunas. The global average is shown by the horizontal red lines.

One could hypothesize that the mean yearly temperature is increasing because of the global warming in both cities. However the conclusion can not be drawn without a statistical test. After performing a Mann-Kendall non-parametric test (code is present in R script) for positive changes over time, in both cases we got p-values lower than 0.05 (for Kaunas < 10e-6, for Madrid < 10e-3) and so we can conclude that the mean yearly temperature in both cities is increasing over time.

It is remarkable to notice that the coldest city (Kaunas) is the one showing the strongest increasing trend.

The plot shows the mean temperature for each month in two cities: Kaunas (Lithuania) and Madrid (Spain).
The data was prepared with R, the script which was used is here.

Both cities follow the same trend: winter time is colder than the summer time. Also, it is interesting that the temperature difference between Kaunas and Madrid is highest in the winter but is way smaller in spring. This reveals that Kaunas temperature increase through the year is bigger than in Madrid, and thus explains the finding from the line plot: colder cities are possibly more sensitive to global warming.
